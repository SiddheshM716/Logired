
"To Kill a Mockingbird" by Harper Lee is a classic novel set in the American South during the 1930s.
The story is narrated by Scout Finch, a young girl, and revolves around her father, Atticus Finch, a
lawyer defending a black man, Tom Robinson, who is falsely accused of raping a white woman. The novel
explores themes of racial injustice, moral growth, and compassion through Scout's coming-of-age journey.
As the trial unfolds, the town's prejudices and social norms are laid bare, challenging the characters
and readers alike to confront issues of racism and morality. The narrative also touches on the importance
of empathy and understanding in the face of deep-seated prejudice. "To Kill a Mockingbird" is celebrated
for its powerful portrayal of social issues and its enduring relevance.
